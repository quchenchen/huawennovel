# Chapter 001 — The Moon Ball

---

She smelled him before I did. That was the first problem.

My wolf had been restless since we crossed the border into Dravon territory — pacing, circling, doing that low whine she only does when something is about to go wrong. I'd spent the last three hours of the carriage ride telling her to stop. She didn't.

The Moon Ball was held in the Dravon palace courtyard, open to the sky, lit by torches and the full moon overhead. Every five years, every pack in the kingdom sent representatives. Every five years, unmated wolves stood under that moon and waited to see if the Goddess had decided something about their lives.

I was here as a representative. Nothing more.

Our pack's Beta — my father, technically, though he'd never used that word for me — had sent me because sending me cost him nothing. I was the lowest-ranking member of the delegation. I carried the documents. I stood at the back.

I was good at standing at the back.

The courtyard was already full when we arrived. I catalogued it the way I always catalogue rooms: exits first, then power centers, then everyone else. Three exits. Power center: the raised platform at the far end, where a man stood with his back to the crowd.

He didn't need to face them. They were already oriented toward him the way iron filings orient toward a magnet — unconsciously, helplessly, like their bodies had made a decision their minds hadn't caught up to yet.

Alpha King Kael Dravon.

I'd heard the name my whole life. Everyone had. I looked at him for exactly two seconds, noted his position, and looked away.

My wolf did not look away.

*Behave,* I told her.

She ignored me.

---

The moon reached its peak an hour into the evening.

I felt it before I saw it — a shift in the air, a collective stillness that moved through the crowd like a held breath. Around me, unmated wolves were lifting their faces, some with hope, some with dread, some with the careful blankness of people who had learned not to want things too visibly.

I kept my eyes on the middle distance. I was here as a representative. I was not here for this.

My wolf slammed into the front of my mind so hard I nearly lost my footing.

*Stop—*

She didn't stop. She never stops when she's certain about something, and she was certain about this with a conviction that felt less like instinct and more like recognition. Like she'd been waiting for this specific moment for twenty-two years and was not interested in my objections.

The bond hit me like a hand closing around my sternum.

Not romantic. Not warm. A *fact* — sudden and structural, like a bone you didn't know you had until someone pressed on it. I felt the direction of it before I found the source, and when I turned, he was already looking at me.

The crowd had parted. I don't know when. People had stepped back without seeming to notice they were doing it, and there was a clear line of sight between me and the man on the platform, who had turned, finally, and whose dark green eyes were fixed on my face with an expression I couldn't read.

He came down from the platform.

The courtyard went quiet in the way that rooms go quiet when the most powerful person in them starts moving with purpose. He walked toward me, and I stood still, because running would have been worse and because my legs had apparently decided to wait and see.

He stopped three steps away.

I could feel the bond pulling at the space between us. My wolf was making a sound I'd never heard from her before — not a whine, not a growl. Something older.

He looked at me for a long moment. His face gave away nothing. His eyes were very dark.

*"I reject this bond."*

He didn't look at me when he said it. He was already turning away.

---

The crowd found its voice again slowly, in pieces — whispers first, then the careful resumption of conversation, the way people talk when they're pretending they didn't just witness something. I stood where I was. My wolf had gone completely silent.

I became aware, gradually, that my hand was at my collarbone. I'd reached for my mother's hairpin without noticing. I made myself lower my arm.

*Practical,* I told myself. *Be practical.*

He had rejected the bond. That was his right. The Moon Goddess ordained the connection; she did not mandate acceptance. It happened. It was over. I would finish the evening, return to the border, and file this under *things that occurred* rather than *things that mattered.*

My wolf did not agree with this categorization. I ignored her.

---

The corridor outside the main courtyard was quieter. I'd slipped out twenty minutes after — not fleeing, I told myself, just done. I had the documents. I had fulfilled my function. There was no reason to stay.

*"Miss Voss."*

I turned.

Lord Cassius Hale was exactly as described in every political briefing I'd ever read: silver-haired, impeccably dressed, the kind of face that had been carefully arranged to suggest trustworthiness over a period of decades. He was smiling at me with what looked like genuine concern.

I noted both, and filed them under *pending.*

*"I hope you'll forgive the intrusion,"* he said, falling into step beside me as though we'd planned to walk together. *"This must be an incredibly difficult evening for you."*

*"I'm fine,"* I said.

*"Of course."* He said it warmly, the way people say *of course* when they mean *you don't have to pretend with me.* He produced an envelope from his jacket — unhurried, like he'd been carrying it for a while. *"I've taken the liberty of arranging a few things. There's a carriage waiting at the east gate. Comfortable. Discreet. And this—"* he pressed the envelope into my hand *"—should cover any immediate needs."*

I looked at the envelope. I looked at him.

*"Why?"* I asked.

*"Because you deserve better than to spend the rest of this evening being stared at."* He said it simply, like it was obvious. *"You're a practical girl. I can see that. Take the carriage. Go home. Let this be a bad night that ends quickly rather than a bad night that goes on."*

I took the envelope.

He smiled again — and that was when I saw it. When I nodded, when the decision was made, his shoulders dropped. Not much. A centimeter, maybe less. The kind of release that happens when a person has been holding tension they didn't realize they were holding.

That wasn't pity.

I didn't know what it was yet. But I filed it away.

*"You're very kind,"* I said.

*"Not at all,"* he said. *"Safe travels, Miss Voss."*

---

The carriage was where he said it would be. Comfortable. Discreet. I sat in the dark as the palace lights fell away behind us, and I turned the envelope over in my hands without opening it.

My wolf was quiet now. The bond was still there — I could feel it, a low persistent pressure behind my ribs, like a bruise that hadn't fully formed yet. I suspected it would feel like that for a while.

I touched my mother's hairpin. Small, silver, worn smooth at the edges from years of handling. She'd left it to me when she died, along with nothing else. I'd never known what it meant to her. I'd never known much about her at all — she'd been gone before I was old enough to ask the right questions, and my father had never been interested in answering them.

I thought about Cassius Hale's shoulders dropping one centimeter.

I thought about him saying *you're a practical girl* — not *you seem practical,* not *I imagine you're practical.* Stated. Certain. Like he already knew.

We had never met before tonight.

I put the envelope in my bag without opening it.

Outside, the moon was still full. The Goddess, apparently, had decided something about my life.

I was going to need to think very carefully about what to do with that.

## 📝 Chapter Summary

### Core Plot
Sera attends the Moon Ball as her pack's lowest-ranking representative. The mate bond activates between her and Alpha King Kael Dravon. He rejects it publicly with four words and no eye contact. Lord Cassius Hale immediately offers her a discreet exit — money, a carriage, a graceful disappearance. She accepts, but notices the one detail that doesn't fit: his shoulders relax the moment she agrees. She files it away.

### Character Notes
- **Sera**: Established as observational, composed, self-directed. Her wolf is introduced as a separate voice. She leaves on her own terms, not because she was broken.
- **Kael**: Present for less than two minutes of page time. Established through other people's reactions before he acts. His rejection is cold not cruel — which is the point.
- **Cassius**: Introduced as helpful and warm. The shoulder detail is the only crack. Most readers will miss it on first read.
