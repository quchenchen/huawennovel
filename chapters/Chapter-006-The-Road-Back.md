# Chapter 006 — The Road Back

---

The bond got stronger as I traveled northwest.

Not dramatically — it wasn't the sudden pull of the Moon Ball, nothing that made me stop walking or catch my breath. It was more like a sound getting louder as you approach its source. Gradual. Consistent. Impossible to ignore once you'd noticed it.

I noticed it on the second day, when I crossed from the eastern lowlands back into the kingdom's central territory. The compass-needle feeling sharpened. The direction became more specific — not just northwest, but *there*, with a precision that was almost spatial, like I could have closed my eyes and pointed.

I didn't close my eyes. I kept walking.

My wolf was calmer than she'd been since the Moon Ball, which I found suspicious. She'd spent four days arguing with me about going east, and now that we were heading back she'd gone quiet and settled, like a dog that had finally been pointed toward home.

*This is not going home,* I told her.

She didn't argue. She just walked.

---

I arrived at the capital's outer district on a Thursday evening, when the light was going amber and the streets were full of people finishing their day. I'd been to the capital twice before — once as a child, once with the delegation — and both times I'd felt the same thing: the particular weight of a place that has been important for a long time and knows it.

The capital was not a comfortable city. It was a significant one.

I found an inn in the outer district, far enough from the palace quarter that no one would think to look for a border pack Beta's illegitimate daughter there. I paid for a week. I gave a false name — Sera Vane, which was close enough to my real name that I'd answer to it without thinking, different enough to not appear in any delegation records.

The innkeeper didn't look at me twice. Good.

---

I sat at the small desk in my room that night and did something I'd been putting off since Ashvale: I wrote it down.

Not everything — not the things that could identify me if the paper was found. But the structure of it, the shape, the way the pieces connected. I'd been carrying it in my head for a week and it was getting crowded in there.

I started with what I knew.

Cassius Hale had known my name before the Moon Ball. He'd had forty-three gold coins prepared for my departure before the bond was identified. His shoulders had dropped when I agreed to leave — relief, not kindness.

My mother was Lena Vael. The Silver Vael were a bloodline in the hills above Ashvale. They were wiped out approximately thirty years ago. Not an accident. My mother was the only survivor. She ran south and hid at the border pack.

Silver doesn't burn the Vael. I have never been burned by silver. I thought this was normal.

The hairpin is old. It's not just silver. Maren didn't know what else it was.

I am, apparently, the last one.

Then what I suspected.

Cassius knew about the Vael bloodline. He knew — or strongly believed — that the Moon Ball would activate a bond between me and the King. He prepared for my removal before it happened, which meant he wanted me gone before I could understand what I was.

The massacre thirty years ago was connected to this. Someone wanted the Vael gone. The same someone, or someone working from the same interest, was still active.

Cassius was the most likely candidate. He'd been in a position of power thirty years ago — young, but not too young. He had the reach. He had the interest in keeping things quiet.

Then what I needed.

Records. The old records Maren had mentioned — someone kept them. In a city like this, records don't disappear; they get buried. The royal archive, the chancellor's records, the old census documents. Something would have the Vael in it. Something would have the order that ended them.

I also needed to understand what the bond meant in this context. A rejected bond doesn't dissolve immediately — I knew that. But I didn't know what it meant for a Vael to be bonded to an Alpha King. If the bloodline was significant enough to be worth massacring, the bond was significant enough to be worth preventing.

Cassius had tried to prevent it by removing me. He'd failed — the bond existed whether I was in the capital or not. Which meant he was going to try something else.

I needed to move faster than he expected.

---

I read back over what I'd written and then I burned it, which was the plan from the start. The act of writing it down was for me, not for the paper. I needed to see it laid out to know if it held together.

It held together.

I sat for a while after the paper was ash, watching the small flame in the candle and thinking about what *faster than he expected* actually meant in practice.

He expected me to be gone. He'd given me money and a route and a graceful exit, and he'd watched me take it. As far as he knew, I was at the border pack, quiet and removed, doing exactly what the forty-three coins had been designed to make me do.

That was an advantage. Small, temporary, but real.

I needed to use it before he found out I was here.

---

The bond was very present that night — not pulling, exactly, more like a pressure, the way you feel a room's warmth before you enter it. The palace was close. He was close. I lay in the dark and was aware of the direction with a specificity that was almost uncomfortable.

My wolf was awake. Not pacing — just present, attentive, the way she gets when something is about to happen.

*Nothing is about to happen,* I told her. *We're going to sleep.*

She didn't believe me.

She was probably right not to.

I closed my eyes and thought about records. About archives. About the kind of paper trail a massacre leaves when the people doing it are careful but not careful enough.

Someone always keeps records.

I was going to find them.
