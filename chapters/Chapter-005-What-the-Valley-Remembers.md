# Chapter 005 — What the Valley Remembers

---

She lived at the edge of town in a house that looked like it had decided to become part of the hillside and was most of the way there. Stone walls, a low roof, a garden that had gone wild in the way gardens go wild when the person tending them has stopped caring about tidiness and started caring only about what grows.

I found her on the third day, which was the day I'd told myself I would leave if I hadn't found anything.

I'd been asking careful questions — not about my mother, not anymore, not after the fence-mender. I'd been asking about the hills. About what had been up there. About the winter it had ended. Most people changed the subject. One woman at the bakery had looked at me with something that might have been pity and said, *"You want to talk to Maren. She's the only one left who'll say anything."*

She'd given me directions. *Turn left at the old mill. Go until the road bends.*

---

Maren was old in the way that some people are old — not diminished, just compressed, like everything unnecessary had been worn away and what remained was entirely essential. She was sitting in her garden when I arrived, doing nothing in particular, and she looked at me the way the fence-mender had looked at me: checking something against a memory.

She didn't look at my face first.

She looked at the hairpin.

*"I wondered,"* she said, *"if one of you would come eventually."*

I stayed very still. *"One of who?"*

*"Sit down,"* she said, and gestured at a low stone wall beside her. *"You've been walking since dawn. I can tell."*

I sat.

She looked at the hairpin for another moment, then looked at my face. *"Your mother was Lena Vael,"* she said. *"Before she was Lena Voss. Before she ran."*

*"Vael,"* I repeated.

*"The Silver Vael."* She said it the way you say a name that used to mean something. *"That was what they were called, in the hills. The ones who carried the old blood."*

My wolf went very still.

*"What old blood?"* I asked.

Maren looked at me for a long moment. *"The kind that doesn't forget what it is,"* she said. *"Even when the people carrying it do."*

---

She didn't tell me everything. I understood, later, that she couldn't — not because she was protecting herself, but because she didn't know everything. She knew what her mother had told her, and her mother had known what she'd seen, and what she'd seen was the end of it rather than the whole of it.

What she told me:

The Silver Vael had lived in the hills above Ashvale for as long as anyone could remember. They were wolves, but not like other wolves — something older, something that had been in the bloodline so long it had become structural. They didn't shift the same way. Their wolves were different. And they had a particular relationship with silver that no one else had.

*"Silver doesn't burn them,"* Maren said. *"It never did. That's how you knew."*

I thought about the hairpin. About the way I'd worn it my whole life without thinking about it. About the way other wolves flinched from silver and I never had.

I'd thought that was normal. I'd thought I was just — less sensitive. Less reactive.

*"What happened to them?"* I asked, though I already knew the shape of the answer.

*"One winter,"* Maren said. *"About thirty years ago. They were gone by spring."*

*"Not an accident,"* I said.

She looked at me sharply. *"Who told you that?"*

*"Someone at the market."*

She was quiet for a moment. *"No,"* she said finally. *"Not an accident. Someone wanted them gone. Someone with enough reach to make it happen quietly and enough interest in keeping it quiet afterward."* She paused. *"Your mother was the only one who got out. She was young — barely more than a girl. She ran south and she didn't stop running until she was somewhere no one would think to look for her."*

*"The border pack,"* I said.

*"The border pack,"* she agreed.

---

I sat with that for a while. The garden was quiet around us, the morning light doing the thing it did in this valley — silver and low, catching the mist.

*"She never told me,"* I said.

*"She wouldn't have,"* Maren said. *"She spent twenty years making sure no one knew. She would have told you when you were older, I think. When she thought it was safe."*

*"She died when I was six."*

Maren's expression shifted. *"I know,"* she said quietly. *"I heard."*

Something in her voice made me look at her more carefully. *"You knew her."*

*"I knew her mother. And I knew Lena, a little, before she left."* She paused. *"She was a serious child. Careful. She noticed things."*

I didn't say anything.

*"You have her eyes,"* Maren said. *"And her way of sitting very still when she's thinking."*

I hadn't realized I was sitting still. I made myself move — shifted my weight, looked down at my hands.

The hairpin was in my hair. I reached up and took it out, held it in my palm the way I had in the inn, and held it up to the morning light.

The silver glow. The way it held the light rather than reflected it.

*"Is this—"* I started.

*"Yes,"* Maren said, before I finished. *"It's old. Older than your mother, older than her mother. It's been in the Vael line a long time."* She paused. *"It's not just silver. But I don't know what else it is. That knowledge went with the ones in the hills."*

I closed my fingers around it.

*"If you have that pin,"* Maren said, *"you're the last one."*

She said it simply, without drama, the way you state a fact that has been true for a long time and has stopped feeling like news.

I sat with it anyway.

The last one.

My wolf made a sound I'd never heard from her before — low, resonant, something between grief and recognition. Not the pacing of the bond. Not the alert of danger. Something older than both of those.

*I know,* I told her. *I know.*

---

Maren stood up, slowly, with the careful movements of someone who has learned to be patient with their own body.

*"I've told you what I know,"* she said. *"Which isn't much. The rest of it — why they were killed, who ordered it, what the Vael blood actually means — that's not in this valley anymore."* She looked at me. *"If you want those answers, you'll have to go somewhere else."*

*"Where?"* I asked.

*"The capital,"* she said. *"The old records. Someone kept records — someone always keeps records. And whoever ordered it thirty years ago is probably still there, still keeping things quiet."* She paused. *"Be careful."*

*"I will,"* I said.

She looked at me the way the fence-mender had looked at me — not unkind, not threatening. Just honest.

*"Your mother was careful too,"* she said. *"It wasn't enough."*

She went inside and closed the door.

---

I stood in her garden for a moment after the door closed.

The capital.

The bond pulled northwest. The capital was northwest.

I hadn't planned to go back. I'd been going east specifically to avoid going back. But the thread led where it led, and I'd known, somewhere underneath the decision to go east, that it was always going to end up pointing the same direction.

I pinned the hairpin back in my hair.

*Fine,* I told my wolf. *Northwest.*

She didn't say *I told you so.*

She didn't have to.
