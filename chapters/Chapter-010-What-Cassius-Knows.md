# Chapter 010 — What Cassius Knows

---

The message came before dawn.

A folded note slipped under my door — not Cassius's handwriting this time. Cleaner, more deliberate. Three lines.

*Cassius has requested an urgent audience. I've delayed it until this evening. You have until then.*

No signature. But I knew the handwriting now.

I sat on the edge of the bed and read it twice and thought: one day. One day to find something that could be used, or to build something that couldn't be ignored. One day before Cassius walked into that audience and told whatever story he'd been preparing for thirty-one years.

I got dressed and went to find Mira.

---

She was in the kitchens when I arrived, and she looked at my face and said, *"How long do you have?"*

*"Until this evening."*

She was quiet for a moment. Then: *"The vault."*

*"Yes."*

*"I can't get you in there,"* she said. *"I don't have access. No one below senior staff does."*

*"Who has access?"*

*"The head archivist. The King's steward. The King."*

I thought about that. *"The King's steward."*

*"He's been with the palace for forty years,"* Mira said. *"He's not going to help a stranger."*

*"What about someone the King sent?"*

She looked at me. *"Do you have something from the King?"*

I didn't. But I had something better — I had a morning, and I had the garden, and I had the fact that Kael Dravon had said *we're going to have to solve it together* and meant it.

*"Not yet,"* I said. *"Give me an hour."*

---

The garden was empty at that hour, but the message I sent through Mira's network of palace staff reached him. He came to the small room off the kitchens rather than the garden — more discreet, less visible — and he arrived alone, which I was starting to understand was how he preferred things.

He read the note I'd written. He was quiet for a moment.

*"The vault,"* he said.

*"The artifacts from the hill settlements. If there's anything that proves what the Vael were — what the bloodline actually means — it's there. And if there's proof of what Cassius ordered, it might be there too. He collected everything. He wouldn't have destroyed it."*

*"Why not?"*

*"Because he's careful,"* I said. *"Careful people keep things. They keep them because they think they're the only ones who know where they are, and having them is power, and destroying them feels like losing something."*

Kael looked at me for a moment. *"You've thought about how he thinks."*

*"I've been thinking about it for two weeks."*

He wrote something on the back of my note and handed it back to me. *"Give this to the steward. He'll take you in."*

I looked at what he'd written. Four words and a seal.

*"This is your authority,"* I said.

*"Yes."*

*"If Cassius finds out you authorized this—"*

*"He'll find out anyway,"* Kael said. *"He finds out everything, eventually. The question is whether we have something to show for it when he does."*

---

The steward's name was Aldric, and he had the particular quality of very old palace staff: he had seen enough that nothing surprised him, and he had served long enough that his loyalty was to the institution rather than to any individual within it. He read Kael's note, looked at me, looked at the hairpin, and said nothing for a long moment.

Then he said, *"Follow me."*

The vault was underground, which I hadn't expected — I'd imagined something more formal, more visible. But it was a practical space: stone walls, careful shelving, objects wrapped in cloth and labeled in a hand that had changed several times over the decades. Aldric led me to a section near the back and stopped.

*"These came in thirty-one years ago,"* he said. *"I was junior staff then. I remember because the order was unusual — not a standard acquisition, no provenance documentation. Just: store these, seal the record."*

*"Cassius's order?"*

He looked at me. *"I don't know whose order. I was told not to ask."*

He left me with a lamp and the instruction to be careful.

---

There were eleven objects. I unwrapped each one carefully, set it on the shelf beside its cloth, and looked at it.

Most of them I couldn't identify — small things, worn smooth, the kind of objects that accumulate in a community over generations and become significant through use rather than design. A carved wooden disc. A length of braided cord. A small clay figure, old enough that the features had blurred.

The seventh object was a book.

Not large — the size of my hand, bound in something that wasn't quite leather. The pages were thin, the writing inside in a script I didn't recognize. But the cover had a symbol pressed into it: a leaf, narrow and elongated, the same shape as the hairpin.

My wolf made the grief-recognition sound again, low and resonant.

I held the book carefully and turned it over. On the back cover, in a different hand — newer, the ink less faded — someone had written four lines in the common tongue.

*For the one who comes after.*
*The bond is not a chain.*
*The blood remembers what the mind forgets.*
*The pin opens what the key cannot.*

I read it three times.

*The pin opens what the key cannot.*

My wolf had gone completely still.

I looked at the hairpin. I looked at the book. I looked at the book's cover — the pressed leaf symbol — and I thought about what Maren had said: *it's not just silver. But I don't know what else it is.*

I touched the hairpin to the cover.

The symbol lit up. Not dramatically — not light, exactly, but a warmth, a resonance, the way a tuning fork responds to its frequency. The book fell open in my hands to a page near the middle, and the script on that page was different from the rest — cleaner, more recent, written in the common tongue.

*If you are reading this, the bloodline survived. What follows is what Cassius Hale does not want you to know.*

---

I read for as long as I dared.

It wasn't everything — the book was old, and the common-tongue section was only a few pages, written by someone who had known they were running out of time. But it was enough. It was more than enough.

The Vael were not just a bloodline with silver immunity. They were the original witnesses — the ones who had, generations ago, formalized the compact between the Alpha King's authority and something older than the kingdom. The bond between a Vael and an Alpha King was not romantic in origin. It was constitutional. A Vael bonded to the King could call him to account before the old compact — could, in the language of the book, *speak the binding* and require him to answer for actions taken against the compact's terms.

Cassius had ordered the massacre because a Vael had been preparing to speak the binding against the previous king — Kael's father — for actions Cassius had taken in the king's name. Actions Cassius had taken without authorization. Actions that, if the binding had been spoken, would have been laid at the king's feet and required answer.

He'd killed an entire bloodline to prevent one person from speaking four words.

And then he'd spent thirty-one years making sure no one was left who could speak them.

Except me.

---

I wrapped the book back in its cloth. I put it in my bag. I knew I was taking something that wasn't mine to take, and I did it anyway, because the alternative was leaving it here for Cassius to find and destroy when he realized what I'd found.

I thanked Aldric on the way out. He looked at my bag and didn't ask.

Outside, the morning was fully bright, and I had until evening, and I had more than the shape of something now.

I had the words.
