# Chapter 008 — Cassius Moves

---

The note was under my door when I got back.

No envelope. No seal. Just a folded piece of paper with two lines on it, written in that careful, precise hand I now recognized on sight.

*Miss Voss. I know you're here. We should speak before you do something irreversible.*

I stood in the doorway and read it twice. Then I stepped inside, closed the door, and sat on the edge of the bed.

He knew I was here. He'd found me in less than a week, which meant he'd been watching for me — not just in the capital, but on the roads, at the inns, at the border. He'd had people looking. He'd known I might come back.

He'd underestimated me at the Moon Ball. He wasn't making that mistake twice.

I looked around the room. Small desk, single window, door I'd just come through. Nothing disturbed — he hadn't searched it, or if he had, he'd been careful. The envelope at the bottom of my bag was still there. Twelve coins left.

I packed in four minutes. I was out the side door in five.

---

The second inn was smaller, further into the outer district, the kind of place that rented rooms by the night to people who didn't want to be found. I paid cash, gave a different false name, and spent the first hour sitting in the dark thinking about what the note meant.

Not the words — the words were clear enough. The meaning underneath them.

*We should speak before you do something irreversible.*

He wasn't threatening me. He was warning me. Which meant he thought I was about to do something that would be bad for me, not just inconvenient for him. Which meant he thought I was going to go to Kael.

He was right. That was exactly what I was planning.

But the warning implied something else: that going to Kael was dangerous for me specifically. Not just politically complicated — dangerous. Cassius had spent thirty-one years managing this situation. He'd massacred an entire bloodline to manage it. He wasn't going to let a warning note be his only move.

If I went to Kael, Cassius would move against me directly.

I needed to think about what that looked like.

---

He had options. I tried to think through them the way he would — methodically, without sentiment.

Option one: discredit me. I was a border pack Beta's illegitimate daughter with no status, no documentation, and a false name. If I walked into the palace claiming to be the last of a massacred bloodline with evidence against the Lord Chancellor, the most likely outcome was that I'd be removed as a nuisance. He wouldn't even need to do anything — the system would do it for him.

Option two: remove me quietly. He'd tried the graceful version at the Moon Ball. That hadn't worked. The less graceful version was available to a man of his resources. A border pack nobody disappearing in the capital wouldn't generate much attention.

The third option was the most elegant: get to Kael first. Tell him whatever story made me the problem rather than the solution. Kael had already rejected the bond — Cassius just needed to make sure he kept rejecting it, and kept rejecting me along with it.

Option three was the most elegant. It was also the most urgent, because it was the one that closed the door permanently.

I needed to get to Kael before Cassius did.

---

The problem was access.

I was operating under a false name in the outer district of a city where I had no connections, no status, and no legitimate reason to request an audience with the Alpha King. The palace had protocols. The protocols existed specifically to prevent people like me from walking in.

I thought about this for a long time.

Then I thought about Mira.

She was one of the palace attendants I'd met during the Moon Ball delegation — young, efficient, the kind of person who knew how everything worked and was invisible because of it. She'd been kind to me in the way that people are kind when they don't expect to see you again. She'd told me her name. I'd remembered it.

I didn't know if she'd help me. I didn't know if she could. But she was the only thread I had that led inside the palace, and I was running out of time.

---

I found her the next morning at the market near the palace gates, which was where the palace staff did their early shopping. I'd guessed at the timing and gotten lucky — she was there, alone, carrying a basket, and she looked at me with the expression of someone trying to place a face they hadn't expected to see again.

*"Miss Voss,"* she said, when it clicked.

*"Sera,"* I said. *"Just Sera. And I need a favor."*

She looked at me for a long moment. Then she looked at the hairpin — the same way Maren had looked at it, the same way the fence-mender had looked at it. Something shifted in her expression.

*"You should come with me,"* she said. *"Not here."*

---

We sat in a small room off the palace kitchens that smelled of bread and woodsmoke, and I told her enough. Not everything — not the archive, not the order, not Cassius's handwriting. But the shape of it: who I was, what I'd found, why I needed to speak to the King before someone else did.

She listened without interrupting. When I finished, she was quiet for a moment.

*"He doesn't take audiences,"* she said. *"Not since—"* She stopped. *"Not for the past three years."*

*"I know,"* I said. *"I'm not asking for an audience. I'm asking for a way in that doesn't go through the official channels."*

*"That's more dangerous than an audience."*

*"I know that too."*

She looked at me. Then she looked at the hairpin again, and something in her expression settled — not quite resolution, but something close to it.

*"There's a garden,"* she said slowly. *"On the east side of the palace. He walks there in the evenings. Alone — he insists on it. The guards do a perimeter, but the garden itself is unwatched."*

*"Why are you telling me this?"* I asked.

She was quiet for a moment. *"Because my grandmother was from the hill settlements above Ashvale,"* she said. *"And she told me, before she died, that if I ever met someone wearing a silver leaf pin, I should help them."*

I didn't say anything.

*"She didn't explain why,"* Mira added. *"She just said: help them. So."*

She told me which evening, which gate, which path through the garden.

I thanked her. She shook her head, like thanks wasn't the right word for it.

*"Be careful,"* she said. *"He's not — he's not easy to talk to. He hasn't been, for a long time."*

*"I know,"* I said.

I didn't, really. But I was going to find out.
