# Chapter 007 — The Archive

---

Getting into the royal archive took two days and cost me thirty-one of Cassius's forty-three coins.

I thought there was a certain justice in that.

The clerk's name was Edric. He was young, underpaid, and had the particular look of someone who had been told his whole life that the rules existed for good reasons and had recently started to doubt it. I found him at the archive's public counter on the first day, asked about access procedures for historical census records, and watched him explain three different bureaucratic obstacles with the resigned fluency of someone who had explained them many times before.

I came back the next morning with thirty-one gold coins in a cloth pouch and a specific list of what I needed.

He looked at the pouch. He looked at the list. He looked at me.

*"Census records are public,"* he said. *"The supplementary ledgers are restricted."*

*"I know,"* I said. *"That's why I'm talking to you instead of the head archivist."*

He was quiet for a moment. Then he took the pouch and told me to come back at closing time.

---

The archive was cold and smelled of old paper and the particular kind of dust that accumulates in places where things are stored rather than used. Edric led me through three locked doors and into a room I was fairly certain didn't appear on any official floor plan, and left me with a lamp and a stack of ledgers and the instruction to be gone before the night guard made his second round.

I had approximately two hours.

I started with the census records, because that was the safest place to start — public information, technically, just inconveniently organized. The Vael appeared in three consecutive census years, listed under the hill settlements above Ashvale. Occupation: *traditional craft.* Population: growing, then stable, then — in the final year — a notation I had to read twice.

*Relocated. See supplementary record 7-C.*

Not *deceased.* Not *dispersed.* *Relocated.*

Someone had written *relocated* in a census record for a community that had been wiped out. Someone had been careful about the language.

I found supplementary record 7-C in the third ledger I checked.

---

It was a single page. Dated thirty-one years ago, which put it one year before the winter Maren had described. It was an order — not a royal decree, nothing with a seal or a formal heading. Just a page in a supplementary ledger, written in a careful, precise hand.

*Re: the hill settlements, Ashvale district. Relocation to be completed before the winter solstice. No record of origin to be retained in public documents. Supplementary records to be sealed for fifty years.*

No signature. No seal.

But I had spent three days at the Moon Ball carrying delegation documents, and I had a good memory for handwriting, and the careful, precise hand on this page was the same careful, precise hand that had written the border pack's formal introduction letter.

Lord Cassius Hale. Lord Chancellor.

I sat with that for a moment.

*Relocation.* Thirty-one years ago, he'd been — what, thirty? Thirty-five? Young for a Lord Chancellor, but not impossibly so. He'd had enough authority to issue an order. He'd had enough foresight to bury it in a supplementary ledger rather than the main record. He'd had enough patience to wait fifty years for the seal to expire, which meant he'd expected to still be in power when it did.

He'd been right about that.

I read the page again. Then I read it a third time, committing it to memory the way I'd learned to commit things to memory — not word for word, but structure and detail, the things that would hold up under questioning.

I didn't copy it down. I didn't need to. And a copy could be found.

---

I kept looking.

There were three more pages in the supplementary record that referenced the Vael, each more oblique than the last. The second page was a property transfer — the hill settlements' land, transferred to the crown. The third was a notation about *artifacts of uncertain provenance* recovered from the settlements, to be held in the palace vault.

I stopped at that one.

*Artifacts of uncertain provenance.*

The hairpin was in my hair. I reached up and touched it without thinking.

My wolf went very still.

The Vael hadn't just been a bloodline. They'd had things — objects, artifacts, things significant enough that someone had thought to collect them rather than destroy them. Things that were now in the palace vault, thirty-one years later, sealed and catalogued and presumably forgotten.

Or not forgotten. Kept.

I thought about Cassius's careful precision. The forty-three coins. The prepared route. The known name.

He hadn't forgotten. He'd been keeping track of everything — the land, the artifacts, the bloodline. He'd been waiting for the bloodline to surface again, because bloodlines do that. They surface. And when this one surfaced, he'd been ready.

He'd been ready for me specifically.

Which meant he'd known my mother had survived. He'd known she'd had a child. He'd been watching, at some level, for twenty-two years.

---

I left before the night guard's second round, as instructed. Edric was waiting by the side door, and he looked at my face when I came out and didn't ask any questions, which I appreciated.

Outside, the capital was quiet in the way cities are quiet late at night — not silent, but reduced, the daytime noise compressed into something more manageable. I walked back to the inn through streets that were mostly empty and thought about what I'd found.

An order. Cassius's handwriting. Thirty-one years ago.

It wasn't enough to take to anyone. I had no copy, no witness, no way to prove I'd seen it. What I had was knowledge — the shape of what had happened, confirmed. The *why* was still missing. Why the Vael specifically. What the bloodline actually meant, beyond silver immunity and old artifacts. Why a bond between a Vael and an Alpha King was worth preventing.

That answer wasn't in the archive.

I thought about where it might be.

My wolf stirred, and the bond pulled northwest, and I thought: *he knows.*

Kael Dravon. Alpha King. He'd rejected the bond without explanation, without eye contact, in four words. He'd been in that throne room for three years since his mate died, and he'd rejected every bond that had been identified since, and he'd done it without hesitation.

But he'd hesitated with me. I'd seen it — a fraction of a second, barely there. He'd looked at me, and something had shifted in his expression, and then he'd said the four words.

He knew something. Maybe not everything. But something.

I was going to have to talk to him.

I had not planned on talking to him.
