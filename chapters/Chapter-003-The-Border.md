# Chapter 003 — The Border

---

I arrived at the border pack on a Tuesday, which felt appropriate. Nothing important ever happened on a Tuesday. You could slip back into a place on a Tuesday and have it feel like you'd never left.

The pack settlement was the same as I'd left it: low buildings, grey stone, the smell of pine and woodsmoke and the particular kind of quiet that comes from people who have learned to keep their voices down. I'd grown up in that quiet. I'd learned to read it the way other people read faces — the difference between the quiet of a calm evening and the quiet of something being carefully not said.

Today it was the second kind.

Word had traveled faster than I had. Of course it had.

---

My father received me in his study, which was his way of making clear this was official business rather than a homecoming. He was a large man who had learned to make himself seem smaller — a useful skill for a Beta who served an Alpha he didn't entirely trust. He was good at his job. He had never been good at anything else.

He looked at me across his desk and waited.

*"The Moon Ball,"* I said. *"The King identified a mate bond."*

A pause. *"And?"*

*"He rejected it."*

He looked down at his desk. Straightened a paper that didn't need straightening.

*"Are you—"* he started.

*"Fine,"* I said. *"I'm fine."*

He nodded, and I saw it: the small release of tension in his shoulders, the almost imperceptible settling. Relief. Not concern for me — relief that this was not going to become complicated. That I was not going to become complicated.

I recognized the expression. I'd been cataloguing versions of it my whole life.

*"Good,"* he said. *"Then we won't speak of it further."*

*"No,"* I agreed. *"We won't."*

---

My room was exactly as I'd left it. Small, clean, functional — I'd made it functional because I'd never been able to make it feel like anything else. A bed, a desk, a window that faced east. I'd always liked that the window faced east. It meant I woke up with the light coming toward me rather than away.

I sat on the edge of the bed and looked at the wall.

The bond was still there. Northwest, steady, patient in the way that things are patient when they have no concept of giving up. I'd been managing it for four days now. I was getting better at it — not at making it smaller, but at keeping it in the background, the way you learn to stop hearing a sound that never stops.

My wolf was less practiced at this than I was.

She'd been quiet since the hairpin moment in the inn, which was its own kind of unsettling. Her silences were never empty. They were the silences of something waiting to say *I told you so.*

I didn't sleep.

I sat at my desk instead, and I thought about the two things that didn't fit, and I thought about what a person does when they have two data points and no framework to put them in. You find more data. You go somewhere you can find more data.

The border was not that place. The border was where you came to disappear, and I had already decided I wasn't going to do that.

---

By the time the sky started to lighten, I'd made a list.

Not on paper — I'd learned young not to write down things that could be found. In my head, organized the way I organize everything: by what I knew, what I suspected, and what I needed to find out.

What I knew: Cassius Hale had known my name before we met. He had prepared a specific sum of money before the Moon Ball. His shoulders had dropped when I agreed to leave.

What I suspected: he had known, or strongly believed, that the King would identify me as a mate. He had prepared for my removal in advance. The removal was the point, not the kindness.

What I needed to find out: why. Why me specifically. Why prepare at all — a rejected mate bond dissolved on its own eventually, didn't it? Why go to the trouble of ensuring I left quietly?

There was only one place I could think of to start: my mother.

I knew almost nothing about her. She'd died when I was six, and my father had never spoken of her willingly, and I'd learned not to ask. But she'd come from somewhere before the border pack. She'd had a life before she'd been left here with a child and no status and a silver hairpin she wore every day until she didn't.

Someone had known her name too, once. Someone had known enough about her to leave her exactly where she'd be forgotten.

I thought about that for a long time.

---

I packed before breakfast. Not everything — just what I could carry without it looking like I was leaving for good. A change of clothes, my documents, the knife I kept under the mattress, and the envelope at the bottom of my bag that I still hadn't spent.

I left a note on my desk. *Gone to follow up on the delegation report. Back within the month.* Vague enough to be true, specific enough to not invite questions.

The bond pulled northwest, steady as ever.

I went east.

Not because east was where the answers were — I didn't know that yet. But because going northwest meant going back toward the palace, toward Kael Dravon and the thing he'd rejected and the man who'd been so relieved to see me go. And I wasn't ready for any of that. Not yet. Not until I had more than two data points and a feeling I couldn't name.

East was where my mother had come from. She'd mentioned it once, when I was small — a town, a valley, something about the light in the mornings. I hadn't paid enough attention. I'd been six.

I was paying attention now.

I stopped at the door and looked back at the room. The window facing east, the light just starting to come through it. The desk with the note on it. The bed I hadn't slept in.

My wolf stirred.

*I know,* I told her. *I know it's not northwest.*

She didn't argue. That was almost worse — she'd been arguing with me for four days, and now she'd stopped, and the silence felt like something I hadn't earned yet.

I closed the door behind me and walked east into the morning.
