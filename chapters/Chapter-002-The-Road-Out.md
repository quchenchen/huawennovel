# Chapter 002 — The Road Out

---

The bond was still there in the morning. I'd half-hoped it wouldn't be.

I woke in the carriage with my cheek against the window and the pale grey light of early dawn coming through the glass, and the first thing I was aware of — before the stiffness in my neck, before the sound of the wheels on the road — was the direction. A low, persistent pull, like a compass needle that had decided north was somewhere behind me and to the left.

I sat up. I pressed my fingers to my sternum, where the bond had first landed. Nothing visible. Nothing that would show.

My wolf was awake. She'd been awake all night, I suspected — I'd slept badly, surfacing every few hours to find her pacing, restless, doing the thing she does when she wants me to turn around.

*We're not turning around,* I told her.

She didn't answer. She just kept pacing.

I looked out the window at the passing trees and made a decision: I would treat the bond the way I treated weather. It was real. It was present. It was not, in any meaningful sense, something I could control. I would note it, account for it, and continue.

My wolf's opinion of this plan was not favorable.

I ignored her and watched the trees.

---

The first town was a half-day's ride from the palace — small, unremarkable, the kind of place that exists to serve travelers who need to stop without wanting to be remembered. I had the carriage driver leave me at the inn on the main road. I told him I'd arrange my own transport from here.

He didn't ask questions. Cassius had chosen well.

I took a room, ate something I didn't taste, and sat on the edge of the bed with the envelope in my hands.

I'd been carrying it for almost twenty hours without opening it. That was long enough.

The seal broke cleanly. Inside: a folded note with a single line of directions — the carriage route back to the border, already mapped — and a cloth pouch that clinked when I set it on the bed.

I counted the coins.

Then I counted them again.

Generous people give round numbers. This was not a round number. It was forty-three gold coins — enough to travel comfortably for two months, enough to rent a room somewhere quiet and eat well and not work. Not enough to do anything else. Not enough to hire people, or buy information, or build anything. Enough to disappear. Precisely enough to disappear, and not a coin more.

I sat with that for a moment.

Someone had calculated this. Not estimated — calculated. They had thought about what a border pack Beta's illegitimate daughter would need to quietly remove herself from the situation, and they had provided exactly that amount, and they had had it ready.

Before the Moon Ball.

I put the coins back in the pouch and the pouch back in the envelope, pressed it flat, and tucked it into the bottom of my bag, under everything else.

I wasn't sure yet what I was going to do with that information. But I wasn't going to spend the money. Accepting it felt like agreeing to something I hadn't read the terms of.

---

I didn't sleep well that night either.

I sat by the window instead, watching the town go quiet below me, and I went back over the Moon Ball the way I go over anything that doesn't add up — methodically, without rushing, looking for the thing I'd missed the first time.

The first thing I'd already found: the money. Forty-three coins, calculated in advance.

The second thing came to me around midnight.

*Miss Voss.*

That was what he'd called me. Not *you* or *the girl from the border delegation* or any of the dozen ways a man of his rank might address someone of mine. He'd used my name. My full name, correctly pronounced, without hesitation.

I was the lowest-ranking member of my delegation. I carried documents. I stood at the back. There was no reason — no political reason, no social reason, no reason of any kind — for Lord Cassius Hale, Lord Chancellor of the Dravon Kingdom, to know my name before we met.

Unless he'd looked me up.

Unless he'd known, before the Moon Ball, that I was going to matter.

I turned that over for a long time. It didn't get more comfortable.

Two things that didn't fit. That was enough to stay awake for.

---

The hairpin caught the candlelight when I finally moved to blow out the flame — a small flash at the edge of my vision that made me pause. I picked it up. I'd worn it so long I'd stopped seeing it, the way you stop seeing things that have always been there.

It was small, silver, shaped like a narrow leaf. Worn smooth at the edges from years of handling — mine, and before mine, my mother's. She'd worn it every day, as far back as I could remember. When she died, it was the only thing she left me.

I held it up to the candle.

The light caught it differently than I expected. Not the flat gleam of ordinary silver — something deeper, almost luminous, like the metal was holding the light rather than reflecting it. I turned it slowly. The effect came and went depending on the angle.

Probably just the quality of the metalwork, I told myself. Old silver did strange things in candlelight.

I set it down on the nightstand. *I'll look at it properly in the morning,* I thought.

I blew out the candle.

I didn't look at it in the morning. I pinned it back in my hair without thinking, the way I always did, and I went downstairs and arranged a horse for the rest of the journey, and I didn't think about the way the light had moved through it until much later.

But my wolf had seen it.

She'd gone very still when I held it up to the flame — not the restless pacing of the night before, not the low whine of something wrong. Something different. Something that felt, from the inside, uncomfortably like recognition.

I didn't ask her what she recognized.

I wasn't sure I was ready for that answer yet.
