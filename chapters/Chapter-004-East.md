# Chapter 004 — East

---

My mother's town didn't have a name on any map I'd seen. It had a name the locals used — Ashvale — but it was the kind of name that existed only in the mouths of people who lived there, passed down the way directions are passed down: *turn left at the old mill, go until the road bends, you'll know it when you see it.*

I knew it when I saw it.

Not because I recognized it. I'd never been here. But there was something about the light — the way it came through the valley in the early morning, low and silver, catching the mist off the river — that felt like something I'd been told about without being told. Like a description I'd absorbed so young I'd forgotten it was a description.

My mother had mentioned the light. I'd been six. I hadn't known I was memorizing it.

---

The town was small. One main road, a market square, a handful of streets branching off like afterthoughts. The kind of place where everyone knew everyone and strangers were noted without being acknowledged — you felt the attention without seeing it, the way you feel a room's temperature before you find the source.

I found a room at the inn, paid for three nights, and spent the first day doing nothing more than being present. Walking the market. Eating at the common table. Letting people get used to my face before I started asking questions.

On the second day, I asked about my mother.

Her name had been Lena. Lena Voss — she'd taken my father's name when she went to the border pack, which meant her name before that was something else. I didn't know what. That was the first thing I needed to find out.

I asked the innkeeper first, because innkeepers know everything and are usually willing to trade information for the appearance of casual conversation. I mentioned my mother's name — Lena, from this valley, about thirty years ago — and watched his face.

He went still for just a moment. Then he smiled and said he didn't recall anyone by that name, and asked if I wanted more bread.

I said yes to the bread. I noted the stillness.

---

The second person was an older woman at the market, selling dried herbs. I bought a small bundle of something I didn't need and mentioned my mother's name in the same casual way, watching her hands while I talked.

Her hands stopped moving.

She looked at me — really looked, the way people look when they're checking something against a memory — and then she looked away and said she'd been here a long time but couldn't place the name. Her voice was steady. Her hands had not resumed moving.

I thanked her and left.

---

The third person was a man repairing a fence at the edge of town. I almost didn't stop — he looked like someone who wouldn't talk to strangers on principle — but my wolf had gone alert when we passed him, that low focused attention she gets when something is relevant.

I stopped.

I asked about my mother.

He didn't go still. He didn't look away. He looked directly at me and said, *"You should leave this town, girl. Go back where you came from."*

Not unkind. Not threatening. The way you tell someone to step back from a ledge — not because you want them gone, but because you can see something they can't.

*"Why?"* I asked.

He looked at me for a long moment. His eyes went to my hair — to the hairpin, I realized. He was looking at the hairpin.

*"Because people who ask about that family don't tend to have quiet lives afterward,"* he said. *"And you look like someone who's had enough trouble already."*

He went back to his fence.

I stood there for a moment, the morning light silver around me, my wolf very still and very certain.

*That family.*

Not *that woman.* Not *that name.* 

*That family.*

---

I went back to the inn and sat on my bed and thought about that for a long time.

*That family.* Not a woman. A family — with enough history that a man repairing a fence thirty years later still knew to be careful about it.

My mother had left this town. Or been sent from it. Or run.

She'd ended up at the border pack with no status, a child, and a silver hairpin she wore every day.

I took the hairpin out of my hair and held it in my palm.

*That family.*

My wolf made a sound I was starting to recognize — not the pacing restlessness of the bond, not the sharp alert of danger. Something quieter. Something that felt like the moment before a door opens.

I closed my fingers around the hairpin.

I wasn't leaving Ashvale. Not yet. There was a family here, and my mother had been part of it, and someone had gone to considerable trouble to make sure that connection stayed buried. Cassius Hale had known my name before the Moon Ball. He had prepared for my removal. He had known I was going to matter.

The only reason I would matter — the only reason a Lord Chancellor would bother with a border pack's illegitimate nobody — was if I was something other than what I appeared to be.

I was starting to think I might be.

---

I went back to the market in the afternoon and bought more things I didn't need from the woman with the herbs. I didn't ask about my mother again. I asked about the valley — how long she'd lived here, what the winters were like, whether the town had always been this size or if it had been larger once.

She relaxed, the way people relax when the dangerous question seems to have passed.

*"It was larger once,"* she said, after a while. *"A long time ago. There was a — a community, you might say. Up in the hills. They kept to themselves mostly. Then one winter they were just gone."*

*"Gone how?"* I asked.

She looked at her herbs. *"Gone,"* she said again, and her voice had gone careful. *"It was a long time ago. Before my time, even. I only know what my mother told me."*

*"What did she tell you?"*

The woman looked at me. Then she looked at the hairpin.

Her expression shifted — something between recognition and fear, there and gone so fast I almost missed it.

*"That it wasn't an accident,"* she said quietly. *"That's all she told me. That it wasn't an accident."*

She started wrapping her herbs again. The conversation was over.

I walked back to the inn with the afternoon light going gold around me and my wolf so still she barely felt present, and I thought: *not an accident.*

A community in the hills. Gone one winter. Not an accident.

My mother had come from here. She'd worn a silver hairpin every day of her life. She'd died when I was six, and my father had never spoken of her, and a Lord Chancellor had known my name before we met and had prepared money for my disappearance in advance.

I had more than two data points now.

I had the shape of something. I didn't have a name for it yet.

But I was going to find one.
