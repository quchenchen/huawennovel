# Chapter 5 — The Capital

I arrived in the capital under a false name.

Sera Vane — close enough to my real name that I'd answer to it without thinking, different enough to not appear in any delegation records. I paid for a week at an inn in the outer district, far enough from the palace quarter that no one would think to look for a border pack nobody there. I paid cash. I kept the knife in my boot and the hairpin in my hair and the twelve remaining coins in a pouch inside my shirt.

The bond was very present. The palace was close. I was aware of the direction with a specificity that was almost uncomfortable — not just northwest, but *there*, precise enough that I could have closed my eyes and pointed.

I didn't close my eyes. I had work to do.

---

Getting into the royal archive took one day and cost me eight of the twelve coins.

The clerk's name was Edric. Young, underpaid, the look of someone who had recently started doubting that the rules existed for good reasons. I found him at the archive's public counter, asked about access procedures for historical census records, and watched him explain three bureaucratic obstacles with the resigned fluency of someone who'd explained them many times.

I came back the next morning with eight coins and a specific list.

He looked at the coins. He looked at the list. He looked at me.

*"Census records are public,"* he said. *"The supplementary ledgers are restricted."*

*"I know,"* I said. *"That's why I'm talking to you."*

He took the coins and told me to come back at closing time.

---

The archive was cold and smelled of old paper. Edric led me through three locked doors and left me with a lamp and a stack of ledgers and two hours before the night guard's second round.

I started with the census records.

The Vael appeared in three consecutive years, listed under the hill settlements above Ashvale. Occupation: *traditional craft.* Population: growing, then stable, then — in the final year — a notation I had to read twice.

*Relocated. See supplementary record 7-C.*

Not *deceased.* Not *dispersed.* *Relocated.*

Someone had been careful about the language.

Supplementary record 7-C was a single page. Dated thirty-one years ago. An order — no royal seal, no formal heading. Just a page in a supplementary ledger, written in a careful, precise hand.

*Re: the hill settlements, Ashvale district. Relocation to be completed before the winter solstice. No record of origin to be retained in public documents. Supplementary records to be sealed for fifty years.*

No signature.

But I had spent three days at the Moon Ball carrying delegation documents, and I had a good memory for handwriting, and the careful, precise hand on this page was the same hand that had written the border pack's formal introduction letter.

Lord Cassius Hale.

I read it three times. I committed it to memory — not word for word, but structure and detail, the things that would hold up. I didn't copy it. A copy could be found.

I kept looking.

A property transfer: the hill settlements' land, transferred to the crown. And then — a notation that made me stop.

*Artifacts of uncertain provenance recovered from the settlements, to be held in the palace vault.*

I reached up and touched the hairpin without thinking.

My wolf went very still.

*Artifacts.* Not destroyed — kept. Collected and stored, thirty-one years ago, by a man who was careful about everything.

He hadn't forgotten. He'd been keeping track — the land, the artifacts, the bloodline. Waiting for the bloodline to surface again, because bloodlines do that. And when it surfaced, he'd been ready.

He'd been ready for me specifically.

Which meant he'd known my mother had survived. He'd known she'd had a child. He'd been watching for twenty-two years.

---

I left before the night guard's second round. Outside, the capital was quiet. I walked back to the inn and thought about what I had.

An order. Cassius's handwriting. Thirty-one years ago.

Not enough to take to anyone. No copy, no witness, no way to prove I'd seen it. What I had was knowledge — the shape of what had happened, confirmed. The *why* was still missing. Why the Vael specifically. What the bloodline actually meant. Why a bond between a Vael and an Alpha King was worth preventing.

That answer wasn't in the archive.

I thought about who had it.

Kael Dravon. Alpha King. He'd rejected the bond without explanation, in four words, without eye contact. But he'd hesitated — a fraction of a second, barely there. He'd looked at me, and something had shifted, and then he'd said the four words.

He knew something.

I was going to have to talk to him.

I had not planned on talking to him.

---

*She had the shape of it. She needed the why. And the why was in the palace.*
