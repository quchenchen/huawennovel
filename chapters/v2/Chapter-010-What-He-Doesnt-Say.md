# Chapter 10 — What He Doesn't Say

Cassius was in the receiving room when we came downstairs.

He looked exactly as he had at the Moon Ball — elegant, controlled, nothing out of place. He smiled when he saw Kael. Then he saw me, one step behind, and the smile stayed exactly the same.

That was the tell. A real smile changes when something unexpected happens. His didn't.

He'd known I was here. He'd known I was in the palace. He'd come anyway, which meant he'd decided the direct approach was worth the risk, which meant he thought he still had the advantage.

*"Your Majesty,"* he said. *"I wasn't aware we had a guest."*

*"Miss Voss is under my protection,"* Kael said. His voice was flat, even. *"She'll be staying."*

Something moved behind Cassius's eyes. Gone before I could name it.

*"Of course,"* he said warmly. *"How fortunate."*

---

The audience was about trade routes. Cassius had prepared documentation, had figures, had a reasonable case for adjusting the eastern tariffs. He presented it efficiently and well, and Kael listened and asked questions, and I stood near the window and watched Cassius the way I'd watched the innkeeper and the herb woman and the fence-mender.

He was good. He was very good. Every few minutes he'd glance at me — not obviously, just a flicker, the way you check on something you're monitoring — and each time his expression was perfectly pleasant.

But his hands were too still.

Cassius Hale was a man who used his hands when he talked — small gestures, precise, controlled. I'd watched him at the Moon Ball. I'd watched him in the corridor when he gave me the envelope. His hands moved.

Right now they were folded on the table and not moving at all.

He was managing something. Something that required effort to contain.

*"The eastern routes have been profitable for three consecutive years,"* he was saying. *"Adjusting the tariff structure now would—"*

*"We'll review the documentation,"* Kael said. *"I'll have a response by the end of the week."*

A pause. Cassius smiled. *"Of course."*

He gathered his papers. He stood. He looked at me one more time — directly, this time, not a flicker — and said, *"Miss Voss. I hope you're finding the capital comfortable."*

*"Very,"* I said. *"Thank you for asking."*

His smile didn't change. *"Do let me know if you need anything."*

He left.

---

Kael waited until the door closed. Then he turned to me.

*"His hands,"* I said, before he could speak.

*"Yes,"* he said. *"I noticed."*

*"He's managing something. He came here knowing I was in the palace, knowing you'd put me under your protection, and he sat through that entire meeting with his hands folded."*

*"He's deciding,"* Kael said.

*"Between which options?"*

*"Between moving against you directly and waiting to see what you do."* He paused. *"He won't wait long."*

I thought about the note under my door. *We should speak before you do something irreversible.* He'd been trying to give me a chance to leave quietly. That window was closed now — I was in the palace, under the King's protection, with the bond accepted. He knew I wasn't leaving.

*"Tonight,"* I said. *"We should do it tonight."*

*"The throne room requires a formal convening. I need to call the council."*

*"How long does that take?"*

*"A few hours. I can have them assembled by this evening."*

*"Then this evening."*

He looked at me. The bond was steady between us now — accepted, settled, a warmth that had stopped being a pressure and started being something else. Something I didn't have a word for yet.

*"Are you ready?"* he asked.

I thought about my mother running south with a silver hairpin and a child. I thought about Maren saying *she was careful* and *it wasn't enough.* I thought about thirty people in the hills above Ashvale who had been called *relocated* in a census record.

*"Yes,"* I said.

---

He didn't touch me again. But when we walked back through the corridor, he stayed close — close enough that our arms almost touched, close enough that I could feel the warmth of him, close enough that the bond hummed quietly between us like something that had finally been allowed to breathe.

He didn't say anything about it.

Neither did I.

But when we reached the corridor junction and he turned toward his study, he paused for just a moment — half a second, barely there — and looked back at me.

I looked back at him.

Then we both turned away.

My wolf made a sound that was entirely too smug.

*Don't,* I told her.

She didn't listen. She never listens when she's right.

---

*Tonight. She was going to speak four words that had been waiting thirty-one years to be said.*
