# Chapter 11 — Cassius Visits

He came back that afternoon.

Not for another audience — he didn't request one. He simply appeared in the corridor outside the library where I was waiting for Kael to finish with his council preparations, and he smiled at me like we'd arranged to meet.

*"Miss Voss,"* he said. *"A moment?"*

I looked at him. I looked at the corridor — empty, the nearest guard around the corner. I looked back at him.

*"Of course,"* I said.

---

He walked with me to the window at the end of the corridor, which was his way of making the conversation feel casual. A man who wanted to threaten you took you somewhere private. A man who wanted to warn you found a window with a view.

*"I'll be direct,"* he said, looking out at the courtyard below. *"I know what you found in the archive. I know what's in the vault. And I know what you're planning to do this evening."*

I said nothing.

*"You're going to speak the binding,"* he said. *"You think it will expose me. You think Kael will be required to act on whatever the compact demands."* He paused. *"You're not wrong about the mechanics."*

*"Then what am I wrong about?"*

He turned to look at me. Up close, his eyes were very calm — the calm of someone who has thought through every scenario and found them all manageable.

*"You're wrong about the outcome,"* he said. *"The binding requires the King to answer. It does not require him to act against me. It requires him to acknowledge what happened — and then it requires him to make a judgment."* A pause. *"Kael has no proof. He has your testimony and a book from a vault that I can claim was planted. The binding will be spoken, the compact will be invoked, and then Kael will have to decide whether to act on the word of a border pack nobody against his Lord Chancellor of thirty years."*

He smiled. *"I've served this kingdom faithfully for three decades. I have allies on every council. I have documentation for every decision I've ever made."* The smile didn't reach his eyes. *"You have a hairpin and a book."*

I looked at him.

*"Leave,"* he said. *"Tonight, before the convening. Take whatever Kael has given you and go. I'll let you go. I've always been willing to let you go."*

*"You were willing to let me go because you thought I didn't know anything,"* I said. *"That's not the same thing."*

Something shifted in his expression — the first real crack in the pleasantness. *"Don't mistake my patience for weakness, Miss Voss."*

*"I'm not,"* I said. *"I'm mistaking it for fear."*

The crack widened. Just for a second — a flash of something cold and precise underneath the warmth — and then it was gone, and he was smiling again.

*"Last warning,"* he said pleasantly. *"I hope you'll be sensible."*

He walked away.

I stood at the window and breathed and thought about what he'd said.

He wasn't wrong about the mechanics. The binding required acknowledgment, not action. Kael would have to make a judgment call with no hard evidence, against a man with thirty years of institutional loyalty.

But he'd made one mistake.

He'd told me exactly what he thought my weakness was.

---

I found Kael in his study and told him everything Cassius had said, word for word.

He listened without interrupting. When I finished, he was quiet for a moment.

*"He's right,"* he said. *"About the mechanics."*

*"I know."*

*"The binding requires my judgment. And my judgment, without proof—"*

*"Isn't enough on its own,"* I said. *"I know. But he made a mistake."*

Kael looked at me.

*"He told me he has documentation for every decision he's ever made,"* I said. *"He keeps records. He's kept records of everything — the land transfer, the artifacts, the order. He's kept them because he thinks he's the only one who knows where they are."*

*"The supplementary ledger,"* Kael said slowly.

*"I memorized the order. Word for word. If I speak it aloud during the binding — if I recite it as testimony — it becomes part of the compact record. Permanent. Unalterable."* I paused. *"He can claim the book was planted. He can't claim I fabricated something I recited from memory in front of the full council."*

Kael was quiet for a long moment.

*"It's not proof,"* he said.

*"No,"* I agreed. *"But it's a record. And records have a way of surviving."*

He looked at me with an expression I was starting to recognize — the one that appeared when I'd done something he hadn't expected.

*"This evening,"* he said.

*"This evening,"* I said.

---

*She smiled back. That was when he stopped being polite — and when she stopped being careful.*
