# Chapter 2 — Forty-Three Coins

Generous people give round numbers.

I sat in the carriage with the envelope on my knee and thought about that. Forty-three. Not forty, not fifty. Forty-three gold coins, which was enough to travel for two months, enough to rent a room somewhere quiet and eat well and not work. Enough to disappear.

Precisely enough to disappear. Not a coin more.

Someone had calculated this. Not estimated — *calculated*. They had thought about what a border pack Beta's illegitimate daughter would need to quietly remove herself from the situation, and they had provided exactly that amount, and they had had it ready before the Moon Ball began.

Before the bond was identified.

I put the coins back in the pouch. I put the pouch back in the envelope. I pressed it flat and tucked it under my bag, and I looked out the window at the dark road and thought about the second thing.

*Miss Voss.*

He'd used my name. My full name, correctly pronounced, without hesitation. I was the lowest-ranking member of my delegation — I carried documents, I stood at the back, I was not the kind of person a Lord Chancellor would have reason to know. There was no political reason, no social reason, no reason of any kind for Cassius Hale to know my name before we met.

Unless he'd looked me up.

Unless he'd known, before the Moon Ball, that I was going to matter.

Two things that didn't fit. That was enough.

---

I told the driver to stop at the first town.

He looked at me — I was supposed to be going back to the border, that was what the route in the envelope said — and I looked back at him until he stopped looking at me and pulled over.

*"I'll arrange my own transport from here,"* I said.

He didn't argue. Cassius had chosen well; the man had been paid to deliver me, not to ensure I stayed delivered.

I found an inn, paid for one night with two of the forty-three coins, and sat on the edge of the bed with the knife from my boot in my hand. Not because I expected trouble — not yet — but because holding it helped me think. It always had.

The bond was still there. Northwest, steady, patient. A compass needle fixed on a direction I wasn't going.

My wolf was pacing.

*I know,* I told her. *But we're not going back.*

She didn't agree. She was going to keep not agreeing for a while. I was used to it.

---

I thought about what I knew about my mother.

Almost nothing. She'd died when I was six. My father had never spoken of her willingly, and I'd learned young not to ask. She'd come from somewhere before the border pack — somewhere east, she'd mentioned once, something about the light in the mornings. She'd had no status when she arrived, no pack affiliation, nothing except a child and a silver hairpin she wore every day until she didn't.

The hairpin was in my hair right now. It always was.

I reached up and took it out. Small, silver, shaped like a narrow leaf. Worn smooth at the edges from years of handling — mine, and before mine, hers. I held it up to the candle.

The light caught it wrong. Not the flat gleam of ordinary silver — something deeper, almost luminous, like the metal was holding the light rather than reflecting it.

I'd noticed this before. I'd always assumed it was just old metalwork.

My wolf went very still.

*What?* I asked her.

She didn't answer. She just stayed still, which was its own kind of answer.

I put the hairpin back in my hair. I blew out the candle. I lay down on the bed with my knife under the pillow and the bond pulling northwest and my wolf silent in a way that felt like waiting.

In the morning, I was going east.

My mother had come from somewhere. Someone had known her name, had known her child would matter, had prepared forty-three coins and a route before the Moon Ball even started.

I was going to find out why.
