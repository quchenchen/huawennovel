# Chapter 17 — What Comes Next

The formal recognition took two weeks.

Kael pushed it through the council over the objections of three of Cassius's former allies, who argued that acting on testimony from a binding was unprecedented and potentially destabilizing. Kael pointed out that the compact had been in place for four hundred years specifically to handle situations like this, and that *unprecedented* was not the same as *wrong*, and that if they wanted to argue the point they were welcome to do so in writing, on record, where it would be preserved alongside the testimony.

They did not argue the point in writing.

The formal recognition was entered into the public record on a Tuesday, which felt appropriate. The Silver Vael: a bloodline of the old compact, present in the kingdom for as long as the compact itself, extinguished in an unauthorized action thirty-one years ago. The record named them. It named what had happened. It named Cassius Hale as the person who had ordered it.

It wasn't justice. The people in the hills were still gone. But their name was on record now, in the public record, where it couldn't be sealed or buried or called *relocated.*

I sent a letter to Maren. She wrote back in three lines: *Good. Your mother would have been glad. Come visit when you can.*

I was going to visit when I could.

---

The title was *Vael Witness* — the formal role under the compact, empty for thirty-one years. It came with a small office in the palace's east wing, a modest stipend, and the right to be present at any convening where the compact was invoked.

It also came with a room in the palace. Permanently.

Next to Kael's.

I moved in on a Wednesday. Mira helped me carry the bag, which didn't take long because I didn't have much. The knife went under the mattress. The hairpin went on the nightstand. The four remaining coins from Cassius's envelope went into a small dish on the desk — I'd decided to keep them, not to spend them. A reminder.

*"You're really staying,"* Mira said, looking around the room.

*"I'm really staying,"* I said.

She smiled. *"Good."*

---

Cassius's review was ongoing. His allies were fighting it, as predicted, and it was going to take months, possibly longer. He was confined to the palace — comfortable confinement, because he was still technically Lord Chancellor until the review concluded, which was its own kind of absurdity. I passed him in the corridor twice. Both times he nodded at me with perfect courtesy.

Both times I nodded back.

I wasn't afraid of him anymore. That was new.

He was still dangerous — I wasn't naive about that. A man like Cassius didn't stop being dangerous because he'd been cornered. But the testimony was on record, the compact held it, and every move he made now was visible in a way it hadn't been before.

He knew that. I could see it in the way he held himself — still controlled, still precise, but something underneath it that hadn't been there before. Something that looked, from the inside of his careful composure, like a man who had spent thirty-one years certain of his position and was now uncertain for the first time.

Good.

---

Kael found me in the garden on the evening of the second week, which was where I'd started going in the evenings, which was where he still went, which meant we were both pretending it was coincidence and neither of us was pretending very hard.

*"The recognition is finalized,"* he said.

*"I know. I was there."*

*"Yes,"* he said. *"You were."*

We walked the garden path together — not touching, just close, the bond warm between us in the evening light. My wolf was very content. She'd been very content for two weeks, which I was choosing to interpret as a good sign rather than a warning.

*"The review,"* I said. *"How long?"*

*"Months. Maybe longer."*

*"And after?"*

*"After,"* he said, *"depends on what the review finds. And on what the council decides. And on a number of things I can't predict."* He paused. *"But the testimony is on record. That doesn't change."*

*"No,"* I said. *"It doesn't."*

We walked in silence for a moment. The garden was the same as it had been the first evening — old, overgrown at the edges, the kind of place that had been tended for generations and then left to find its own shape.

*"I've been thinking,"* he said.

*"About what?"*

*"About the bond. What it means. What it changes."* He paused. *"What I want it to mean."*

I looked at him. *"And?"*

*"I want it to mean something,"* he said. *"Not just constitutionally."*

I thought about that. *"That's a significant statement."*

*"Yes,"* he said. *"It is."*

*"You could have led with that,"* I said. *"Weeks ago."*

*"I could have,"* he agreed. *"I wasn't ready weeks ago."*

*"Are you ready now?"*

He looked at me. The real smile — the one that changed his face, the one I'd been waiting to see since the Moon Ball.

*"Getting there,"* he said.

*"Good,"* I said. *"Me too."*

The bond hummed between us, warm and steady and entirely itself, and the evening light went gold through the garden, and my wolf was so smug I could feel it in my bones.

*Don't,* I told her.

She didn't listen.

She was right not to.

---

*The hairpin was in her hair. The bond was in her chest. The name Vael was on the public record.*

*She was getting there.*

*So was he.*
