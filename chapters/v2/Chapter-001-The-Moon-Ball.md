# Chapter 1 — The Moon Ball

The dress didn't fit right. I'd borrowed it from the pack's communal wardrobe — the kind of wardrobe that exists because border packs can't afford individual formal wear — and whoever had worn it last was two inches shorter than me. The hem sat wrong. I'd pinned it up in three places and hoped no one looked at my ankles.

No one was going to look at my ankles. I was the lowest-ranking member of the Ironveil delegation, which meant I was here to carry documents and stand at the back and not speak unless spoken to. I'd done this before. I was good at it.

The Moon Ball was held in the palace's great hall, which was the kind of room designed to make you feel small. High ceilings, cold stone, a thousand candles doing their best against the dark. The Alpha King sat at the far end on a raised dais — not a throne, exactly, just a chair that had been given enough authority over the years to become one. He wasn't looking at the room. He was looking at nothing, the way people look when they've learned to be present without being available.

I'd been told he was cold. I'd been told he'd rejected every bond identified in the three years since his mate died. I filed both pieces of information under *not my problem* and went to stand at the back with the documents.

That was when it hit me.

---

Not gradually. Not like the stories — the slow warmth, the gentle pull, the sense of *recognition*. This was a fist to the sternum. I actually took a step back.

My wolf went from dormant to *screaming* in under a second.

*No,* I told her. *Absolutely not.*

She didn't listen. She never listens when it matters.

I knew what it was. Everyone in the room knew what it was — the bond doesn't announce itself quietly. The air changed. The nearest delegates stepped back. Someone made a small sound.

The Alpha King had gone very still.

He was looking at me.

Not at the delegation. Not at the room. At me — Sera Voss, border pack Beta's illegitimate daughter, standing at the back with a stack of documents and a borrowed dress with a pinned hem.

I held his gaze for exactly three seconds. Then I looked away first, because I am not an idiot, and because the bond was already pulling hard enough that I needed to do something with my hands. I straightened the documents. I breathed.

The room was very quiet.

---

He walked toward me. The crowd parted — of course it did, he was the Alpha King — and I watched him come and thought: *this is going to be bad.*

He stopped two feet away. Close enough that the bond went from a fist to a full-body pressure, heat spreading up from my sternum to my throat. My wolf made a sound I'd never heard from her before. I kept my face still.

He looked at me for a long moment. Something moved in his expression — there and gone, too fast to name.

Then he said: *"I reject this bond."*

Four words. No eye contact when he said them — he was already turning away.

The room exhaled.

I stood there with the documents in my hands and the bond still burning in my chest — *rejected doesn't mean gone, I knew that, everyone knew that* — and I thought: *all right. Fine. That's fine.*

My wolf disagreed loudly.

*I know,* I told her. *I know.*

---

Lord Cassius Hale found me twenty minutes later, near the side door I'd been edging toward.

He was the King's uncle — Lord Chancellor, the man who ran the kingdom's day-to-day machinery while the King sat on his dais and looked at nothing. He was elegant in the way that very controlled people are elegant: nothing out of place, nothing wasted. He smiled at me like we were old acquaintances.

*"Miss Voss,"* he said.

I noted that he knew my name. I was the lowest-ranking member of the delegation. There was no reason he should know my name.

I filed it.

*"Lord Cassius,"* I said.

*"A difficult evening."* His voice was warm, sympathetic. *"The King's rejection was — abrupt. I apologize on his behalf."*

*"No apology necessary."*

*"Still."* He produced an envelope from his jacket — smoothly, like it had been there all along. *"I've taken the liberty of arranging transport back to the border. And something to ease the inconvenience."* He held it out. *"Consider it a gesture of goodwill."*

I looked at the envelope. I looked at him.

He'd had it ready. Before the ball. Before the bond had been identified. He'd had an envelope prepared for me specifically.

I took it. I opened it right there, in front of him, and counted the coins.

He watched me count. His expression didn't change.

*"Forty-three,"* I said. *"Thank you. That's very kind."*

His shoulders dropped — barely, a millimeter, the kind of release that happens when something you've been holding finally resolves. He smiled again.

*"Safe travels, Miss Voss."*

I walked out the side door with the envelope in my hand and the bond still burning and my wolf still furious, and I thought about that shoulder drop all the way to the carriage.

That wasn't relief that I was leaving.

That was relief that I was going where he'd planned.

---

*She took his money. She just didn't take his exit.*
